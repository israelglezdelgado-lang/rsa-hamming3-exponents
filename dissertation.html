RSA Public Exponents with Hamming Weight 3:
Experimental Study and Viability Analysis

Cryptographic Research

Abstract

This work examines the viability of using, as public exponents in the RSA algorithm, large prime numbers with Hamming weight exactly equal to three. The conventional choice of Fermat primes — particularly e = 65537 — responds to criteria of computational efficiency and regulatory compliance. We investigate whether exponents of greater magnitude, while maintaining low Hamming weight, constitute a valid operational alternative. We describe the experimental generation procedures, the results obtained across different bit ranges, the corresponding combinatorial analysis, and discuss implications for security, interoperability, and compliance with current standards. Additionally, we present the implementation of a Bash script that automates the generation and verification of such primes, incorporating robust argument validation, progress indicators, and execution timing.

Keywords: RSA, public exponent, Hamming weight, Fermat primes, experimental cryptography, FIPS standards, Bash scripting, OpenSSL.

I.Introduction

The RSA algorithm, formulated by Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman in 1977, remains one of the pillars of contemporary public-key cryptography. Its security rests on the computational difficulty of the integer factorization problem and, more precisely, on the difficulty of the RSA problem: computing e-th roots modulo n without knowledge of its factorization.

The generation of an RSA key pair requires the selection of several parameters. Among them, the public exponent e occupies a particular position: it must be coprime with φ(n) — or, more precisely, with λ(n) = lcm(p−1, q−1) — and, for efficiency reasons, it is favored that it possess a binary representation with low Hamming weight. Historically, this combination of requirements has led to the nearly universal adoption of the Fermat primes F₀ = 3, F₁ = 5, F₂ = 17, F₃ = 257, and F₄ = 65537, of which the last has consolidated as the de facto value in the vast majority of implementations.

This study starts from the observation that Fermat primes, and particularly F₄, result small compared to the modulus n, whose recommended sizes currently reach 3072 or 4096 bits. We investigate whether the use of public exponents of greater magnitude, with Hamming weight equal to three, constitutes a technically viable alternative and whether it offers relevant advantages or disadvantages compared to established practice.

II.Theoretical Framework

II.1. The RSA Algorithm

The RSA scheme operates over a modulus n = p·q, product of two large primes. The public key consists of the pair (n, e), and the private key of (n, d), where d is the multiplicative inverse of e modulo λ(n). The fundamental operations are:

  • Encryption: c = me mod n.
  • Decryption: m = cd mod n.
  • Signature: s = hd mod n, where h is the message digest.
  • Verification: h = se mod n.

The public exponent intervenes in encryption and signature verification operations, while the private exponent intervenes in decryption and signature generation. This asymmetry has direct consequences on the impact of the size of e on the overall system performance.

II.2. Criteria for Public Exponent Selection

The selection of e is subject to three categories of constraints:

  1. Mathematical correctness: e must be coprime with λ(n). Given that p and q are prime, it suffices that e does not divide p−1 nor q−1. Choosing e prime simplifies this verification.
  2. Security: small values of e expose to specific attacks — Håstad (1986), Coppersmith (1996), small-message attacks — which do not extend to exponents of adequate magnitude. The value e = 3 has been discouraged for these reasons since the late 1990s.
  3. Efficiency: modular exponentiation via the binary algorithm (square-and-multiply) performs ⌊log₂ e⌋ squares and Hamming(e) − 1 additional multiplications. Minimizing Hamming weight reduces the cost of encryption and verification.

II.3. Fermat Primes

Fermat primes are numbers of the form Fk = 22k + 1. Only five prime values are known: F₀ to F₄. Their binary representation consists of a 1 bit, followed by zeros, and ending in 1; they therefore have Hamming weight equal to 2. The value F₄ = 65537 offers a particularly favorable balance: its magnitude is sufficient to avoid known attacks against e = 3, and its Hamming weight 2 reduces the exponentiation cost to 16 squares and 1 multiplication.

III.Research Hypothesis

The following working hypothesis is posed: it is possible to construct public RSA exponents of large magnitude — comparable in bit order to the modulus n — that maintain low Hamming weight (equal to 3), are prime, and result operationally valid for standard RSA schemes.

The underlying motivation is defensive in nature: given the possibility — not verifiable but historically not unfounded — that unpublished cryptanalytic results exist that compromise RSA for small values of e, the use of exponents of greater magnitude would constitute a reasonable precaution, even at the cost of efficiency and interoperability.

IV.Experimental Methodology

An experimental procedure was designed to generate prime numbers of various magnitudes with Hamming weight exactly equal to 3. The procedure is described below.

IV.1. Candidate Generation

For each considered magnitude k, candidates of k bits are constructed by fixing:

  • The most significant bit to 1, to guarantee that the number has exactly k bits.
  • The least significant bit to 1, to guarantee that the number is odd — a necessary condition for primality, except for the trivial case of 2 —.

With these two restrictions, the third active bit must be placed in one of the k − 2 intermediate positions. The total number of candidates is therefore k − 2, and each is subjected to a primality test.

IV.2. Primality Verification

Primality was verified using the Miller–Rabin test implemented in openssl, using a sufficient number of rounds to guarantee a negligible error probability in the context of the study. The implementation uses hexadecimal mode (openssl prime -hex) for robustness with large numbers.

IV.3. Implementation Script

A Bash script (hamming3_primes.sh) was developed to automate the generation process. The script incorporates the following technical features:

  • Robust argument validation: verifies that the input is a positive integer, enforces minimum value (N ≥ 3), and provides confirmation prompts for very large values.
  • Progress indicator: reports evaluation progress every 100 candidates, displaying elapsed time and primes found so far.
  • Execution timing: measures and displays total runtime upon completion.
  • Prime counter: reports the total number of primes found at the end of execution.
  • Native binary-to-decimal conversion: uses Bash arithmetic ($((2#$candidate))) instead of external tools like bc, improving performance and eliminating dependencies.
  • Help option: -h or --help displays usage information and examples.

IV.4. Operational Validation

The obtained primes were used as public exponents in RSA key generation, and their correct operation was verified through encryption, decryption, signature, and verification operations using openssl. Additionally, attempts were made to use them in PDF document signing via Adobe Acrobat, in order to evaluate interoperability with end-user applications.

V.2. Combinatorial Analysis

According to the Prime Number Theorem, the density of primes around 2k is approximately 1/(k·ln 2). The expected number of primes among the k − 2 candidates is therefore:

E[primes] = (k − 2) / (k · ln 2)

For large values of k, this expression tends to 1/ln 2 ≈ 1.4427. The observed values in Table 1 range from 2 to 6, which, given the small absolute sample size, is statistically compatible with a Poisson process of rate λ ≈ 1.44. No significant discrepancies are observed between the experimental results and the theoretical predictions.
